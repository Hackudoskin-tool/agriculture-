Indian Agriculture: A Deep Dive

India is an agriculturally important country. Two-thirds of its population is engaged in agricultural activities. It is a primary activity, which produces most of the food that we consume.

The Three Sowing Seasons of India

In your textbook, you learned about Kharif and Rabi. However, there is a third season called Zaid.

Season Sowing Period Harvesting Period Major Crops
Kharif June - July (Monsoon) Sept - Oct Rice, Maize, Jowar, Bajra, Cotton
Rabi Oct - Dec (Winter) April - June Wheat, Barley, Peas, Gram, Mustard
Zaid March - June (Summer) June Watermelon, Cucumber, Fodder crops

Major Crop Profile: Rice

It is the staple food crop of a majority of the people in India. India is the second largest producer of rice in the world after China. It is a Kharif crop which requires high temperature (above 25°C) and high humidity with annual rainfall above 100 cm.

Farming Systems: Then vs. Now

Figure 1: Traditional small-scale vegetable patches.

Primitive Subsistence Farming: Practiced on small patches of land with the help of primitive tools like hoe, dao, and digging sticks. It depends upon monsoon and natural fertility of the soil.

Shifting Cultivation (Jhumming): Farmers clear a patch of land and produce cereals and other food crops to sustain their family. When the soil fertility decreases, the farmers shift and clear a fresh patch of land for cultivation.

The Historical Tools of the Trade

  • Wooden Plough: Historically used to till soil and mix organic manure.
  • Persian Wheel: An ancient method used to lift water from open wells using animal power.
  • Sickle: The primary tool for hand-harvesting across the centuries.

Conclusion

While modern technology has increased food production, traditional methods remind us of the importance of Soil Health and Ecological Balance. Transitioning back to organic methods is becoming a global necessity for a sustainable future.
